How ClearLegacy Wills Are Reviewed

Written by: SL · Last updated: May 2026

ClearLegacy is fully automated. Every will runs through a multi-stage quality-assurance check designed against the Wills Act 1837 before it reaches your inbox. Here is exactly what gets checked, and what to do if anything still slips through.

Quick answer

ClearLegacy is an automated service — no human advisor reviews your will line-by-line. Instead, your answers run through eight automated quality-assurance checks (executor validity, beneficiary clarity, residuary cover, guardianship, gift specificity, revocation, attestation wording, and internal consistency). The system flags anything that doesn't add up before generating the document. The output is a Wills Act 1837–compliant will with a signing & witnessing pack, delivered by email.

Why automated, not human-reviewed

Most UK estates are simple. The Wills Act 1837 sets out clear formal requirements: the testator must be 18+, of sound mind, sign the document, and have it witnessed by two independent witnesses present at the same time. A structured questionnaire that asks the right questions in the right order can produce a legally valid will for the great majority of households.

Charging £200–£400 for a high-street solicitor to walk you through executor choices — when the executor field on the form has clear rules attached to it — is mostly paying for office overhead and hourly billing, not legal value. Automating the questionnaire and the quality checks is how we deliver the same legally valid will for £69.

⚠️ When automation isn't enough. If your estate includes discretionary trusts for a vulnerable beneficiary, a family business with other shareholders, significant foreign property, or a second marriage with children from both sides, an automated service is not the right tool. We say this plainly throughout the site and we will refund anyone whose situation is genuinely too complex for the product. See the pricing page for more.

The end-to-end process

  1. Structured questionnaireYou answer a sequence of questions covering executors, beneficiaries, residuary estate, guardians (if relevant), specific gifts, and funeral wishes. Questions branch based on your earlier answers — you never see questions that don't apply to your situation.
  2. Validation at the field levelEach answer is validated as you go. Executor age 18+; beneficiary identification clear enough to be unambiguous; percentages sum to 100% for any split estate; addresses formatted to UK conventions.
  3. Cross-answer consistency checkBefore the will is generated, the system runs an internal-consistency pass: is anyone named twice in conflicting roles? Has a guardian been named for children who weren't declared? Does the residuary clause cover the whole estate? If anything fails, you're asked to clarify.
  4. Will generation against the Wills Act 1837The document is rendered with attestation wording compliant with section 9 of the Wills Act 1837, a revocation clause for any prior wills, and the correct execution block for two independent witnesses.
  5. Final QA passThe generated document is checked against the answers one more time before delivery — no unfilled placeholders, no template artefacts, no missing clauses.
  6. Delivery + signing packYou receive a PDF of your will plus a signing & witnessing guide: who can witness, who can't, the order of signing, and the common mistakes that invalidate a will at probate.

The eight quality-assurance checks

Every will runs through these checks before it is generated. If any check fails, the system blocks generation and asks you to clarify the relevant answer.

1. Executor validity

Named executors are 18+, identifiable, and not disqualified (for example, a bankrupt is not blocked from acting but is flagged).

2. Beneficiary clarity

Each beneficiary is identified clearly enough — full name, relationship, and where helpful date of birth or address — that a probate court could not reasonably mistake who is meant.

3. Residuary estate coverage

Percentage splits sum to exactly 100%. Substitution rules are in place if a residuary beneficiary predeceases you.

4. Guardianship

If you have children under 18, at least one guardian is named. Where two guardians are named, the order of appointment is unambiguous.

5. Gift specificity

Specific gifts (jewellery, cars, cash sums, charitable legacies) are described precisely enough to be identifiable, and the source of each cash gift is clear (residuary vs. general).

6. Revocation

A revocation clause is included so that this will supersedes any previous will or codicil.

7. Wills Act 1837 attestation

The execution block satisfies section 9 of the Wills Act 1837: testator signs in the presence of two witnesses present at the same time, who then sign in the testator's presence.

8. Internal consistency

Cross-references between sections (executor also named as beneficiary, guardian also named as executor, etc.) are flagged where they could cause an interpretation issue at probate.

If something is wrong

If you spot a mistake in the PDF — a misspelled name, a transposed digit, a beneficiary in the wrong section — email hello@clearlegacy.co.uk before signing the document. We will regenerate a corrected version free of charge. Once the will has been signed and witnessed it is legally valid; further changes need a codicil or a new will.

Do not amend a signed will by hand. Any handwritten change to a signed will may invalidate that clause — or, in some cases, the whole will. If something needs to change after signing, do a fresh will or a codicil.

FAQs

Who reviews ClearLegacy wills?

ClearLegacy is an automated will-writing service. There is no human advisor reviewing each will line-by-line. Instead, every will is generated from a structured questionnaire and runs through a multi-stage automated quality-assurance process designed and overseen by SL, the founder.

Is an automated review as reliable as a solicitor?

For simple and mid-complexity UK estates, yes. The Wills Act 1837 has clear formal requirements; a structured questionnaire that covers all of them produces a legally valid will. For complex estates — discretionary trusts, family business shares, foreign property, contested second marriages — a solicitor is the right choice and we say so on the relevant pages.

What does the automated review check?

Eight categories: (1) executor validity, (2) beneficiary clarity, (3) residuary estate coverage, (4) guardianship for minor children, (5) gift specificity, (6) revocation of any prior wills, (7) Wills Act 1837 attestation wording, and (8) internal consistency between the answers you gave.

What happens if my answers don't add up?

The system flags it and asks you to clarify before the will is generated. Common flags: residuary estate adding up to more or less than 100%, a guardian named for children who weren't declared, or an executor who is also the sole beneficiary.

What does the will I receive include?

A PDF will drafted to the Wills Act 1837 and a signing & witnessing pack explaining exactly how to execute it: two independent witnesses present at the same time, who can witness, who can't, and the common mistakes that invalidate a will at probate.

What if I find a mistake in my will?

Email hello@clearlegacy.co.uk before signing. We will re-issue a corrected version free of charge. Minor edits (address change, new executor, new child) are free for 12 months after purchase.
