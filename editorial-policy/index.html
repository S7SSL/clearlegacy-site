Editorial Policy

Written by: SL · Last updated: May 2026

How we research, write, and review every guide on ClearLegacy. The sources we trust, the cadence we update at, and how to flag a correction.

Quick answer

ClearLegacy guides are written and reviewed by SL, the founder. We use primary UK sources only — GOV.UK, HMRC, Citizens Advice, the Office of the Public Guardian, and the underlying statute (most importantly the Wills Act 1837). Every guide is reviewed at least annually and immediately when the rules change. Corrections: hello@clearlegacy.co.uk.

What this policy covers

This page describes how the content on clearlegacy.co.uk is produced — the guides, explainers, comparison pages, and FAQs. It does not cover the wills themselves; the end-to-end process for producing a customer's will and the quality checks applied to it are documented separately on the review process page.

Our editorial principles

  1. Factual accuracy first. Every figure, threshold, and rule on the site is checked against a primary UK source at the time of publication. If we can't verify it, we don't publish it.
  2. UK legal compliance. All content is written for England and Wales. Where Scottish or Northern Irish rules differ materially (for example, intestacy), we say so rather than glossing over it.
  3. No legal advice. We publish general guidance, not personal legal advice. The guides explain how UK rules work; what to do in your specific case may need a solicitor, and where we think that's true we say so plainly.
  4. Plain English. The audience is the general public, not the legal profession. We avoid Latin, avoid jargon where a plain term works, and define terms the first time they appear.
  5. No padded copy. If a guide can answer the user's question in 400 words, that's what we publish. We do not pad to hit a word count.

How we research

Every guide begins with the underlying UK source rather than secondary content. In practice that means:

  • Statute — principally the Wills Act 1837, the Administration of Estates Act 1925 (intestacy), the Inheritance (Provision for Family and Dependants) Act 1975, the Mental Capacity Act 2005 (LPAs), and the Inheritance Tax Act 1984.
  • Government guidance — GOV.UK pages for wills, probate, inheritance tax, and lasting power of attorney; the Office of the Public Guardian for LPA-specific guidance; HMRC for IHT thresholds, reliefs and forms; HM Courts & Tribunals Service for probate process.
  • Independent consumer guidance — Citizens Advice, MoneyHelper, the Money Advice Service.
  • Professional bodies — the Law Society, STEP, and the Society of Will Writers for context on professional practice and typical fees.

How we write

Each guide is structured around the question a user is actually asking. We put the answer at the top (the "Quick Answer" block), then explain the underlying rule, then show how it applies in practice, then cite the sources used.

Internal links are used to connect related concepts — for example, an IHT page links to the nil-rate band guide and to the residence nil-rate band guide rather than re-explaining each. We do not link out to commercial competitors; we do link out to UK government and statutory sources where it helps the reader verify a rule.

How we review

Every published guide is reviewed by SL before it goes live. Reviews check four things:

  1. Source accuracyAre the cited rules current, and do the cited URLs still load?
  2. Threshold accuracyFor tax pages, do the IHT thresholds, RNRB, gift exemptions and rates match the latest HMRC position?
  3. ConsistencyDo we describe the same rule the same way across guides? Conflicting language is a red flag for an out-of-date page.
  4. ToneDoes the page give general guidance, not personal legal advice? Have we recommended a solicitor where the situation genuinely needs one?

Update cadence

Every guide is reviewed at least once a year. In practice most are touched more often, because UK estate-planning rules move:

  • IHT thresholds are frozen until 2030 — but the residence nil-rate band, gift exemptions, and reliefs are reviewed annually.
  • APR and BPR face a combined £1m cap from April 2026; affected pages were rewritten in 2025.
  • Pension IHT treatment changes from 2027 — flagged in the relevant guides as of May 2026.

The Last updated date on each guide reflects the most recent meaningful edit, not a cosmetic touch.

Corrections

If you find an error, a broken citation, or an outdated threshold on any guide, please email hello@clearlegacy.co.uk with "content correction" in the subject line and a link to the page. We aim to verify and either correct or respond within five working days. Material corrections are noted in the page footer where they affect the underlying advice.

Conflicts of interest

ClearLegacy sells online wills. Where a guide compares us to a competitor, that comparison is based on published prices and product features at the time of writing, with sources cited. We do not accept paid placement, sponsored content, or affiliate fees from other will providers, banks, or insurance companies.

FAQs

Who writes ClearLegacy's guides?

All guides are written and reviewed by SL, the founder of ClearLegacy. Where a guide references a specific UK rule, the underlying source is cited.

What sources does ClearLegacy use?

Primary UK sources only: GOV.UK, HMRC, Citizens Advice, the Office of the Public Guardian, the Law Society, the Probate Registry, and the underlying statute — most importantly the Wills Act 1837. We do not rely on aggregated third-party content.

How often is content updated?

Each guide is reviewed at least annually and immediately when a relevant UK rule changes. Recent triggers include the IHT threshold freeze to 2030, the APR/BPR £1m cap from April 2026, and the pension IHT changes announced for 2027.

How do I report an error?

Email hello@clearlegacy.co.uk with "content correction" in the subject line and a link to the page. We aim to verify and either correct or respond within five working days.

Does ClearLegacy give legal advice?

No. ClearLegacy publishes general guidance about UK estate planning and provides an online will-writing service. The guides explain how the rules work; they are not personal legal advice. For complex estates we recommend a qualified solicitor.
