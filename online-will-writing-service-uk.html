Security and Legal Validity

Is an online will legally valid?

Yes. The Wills Act 1837 governs validity in England and Wales and does not specify who must draft the document. What it does specify is how the will must be executed: in writing, signed by you, witnessed by two non-beneficiary adults present at the same time. An online will writing service produces the document; the signing step happens in person.

How is my data protected?

ClearLegacy uses encrypted connections (HTTPS) for every step, stores submitted data securely in UK / EU data centres, and follows UK GDPR. Your data is used only to draft and deliver your will — it is never sold or shared. You can request deletion at any time.

Probate registries don't ask who drafted the will. They check that it complies with the Wills Act 1837. A correctly executed online will is admitted to probate in exactly the same way as a solicitor will.

When an Online Service Isn't the Right Choice

An online will writing service handles the vast majority of UK estates. Use a specialist solicitor instead if you:

  • Want to set up a discretionary or life-interest trust
  • Are planning business succession for a company you control
  • Hold significant property abroad in jurisdictions with forced-heirship rules
  • Expect the will to be contested
  • Are using your will as part of an inheritance tax planning strategy
  • Have concerns about your mental capacity being challenged after death
