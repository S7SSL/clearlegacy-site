What Is an Online Will?

An online will is the same legal document as any other will — it just gets there a different way. Instead of sitting across a desk from a solicitor, you answer plain-English questions through a secure online form. The system uses your answers to draft a will that complies with the Wills Act 1837, the same law every UK will is written under.

You then print the will, sign it in front of two adult witnesses (who must not be beneficiaries), and they sign in your presence. That signed paper document is your legal will.

Key point: "online will" describes how the document is drafted. Once it's signed and witnessed correctly, it carries identical legal weight to a £500 solicitor will.

What You Can Include in an Online Will

An online will covers everything a solicitor's will does, including:

  • Property — your home, second homes, buy-to-let, holiday property
  • Savings and investments — bank accounts, ISAs, premium bonds, shares
  • Pensions and life insurance (where they don't pass via nomination)
  • Specific gifts — jewellery, vehicles, family heirlooms
  • Pecuniary gifts — fixed cash sums to named people
  • Charitable legacies — gifts to UK charities (exempt from inheritance tax)
  • Residuary estate — everything else, usually the largest part
  • Executors — up to four people who carry out your wishes
  • Guardians for children under 18
  • Funeral wishes — burial or cremation preferences

Is an Online Will Legal?

Yes — and the law is unambiguous on this. The Wills Act 1837 sets four requirements, and none of them mention solicitors or drafting method:

  1. The will is in writing
  2. You sign it (or someone signs at your direction in your presence)
  3. Two adult witnesses are present at the same time when you sign
  4. Those witnesses sign in your presence

The witnesses cannot be beneficiaries or married to a beneficiary. You must be 18 or over with the mental capacity to understand what you are doing. That is the entire test.
