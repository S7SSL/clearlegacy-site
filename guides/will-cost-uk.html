How Much Does a Will Cost in the UK? (2026 Guide)

Last updated: April 2026 · Reading time: 8 minutes

Writing a will is one of the most important things you can do to protect your family — but how much should you actually pay? Will costs in the UK vary enormously, from free DIY templates to £1,000+ solicitor fees. This guide breaks down every option so you can make the right choice for your circumstances.

How much does a solicitor will cost?

A solicitor-drafted will in the UK typically costs between £150 and £500 for a single will, and £250 to £800 for mirror wills (matching wills for couples).

Prices vary significantly by location. London solicitors commonly charge £300–£600 for a straightforward single will; firms outside the capital may charge £150–£350.

For more complex estates — those involving trusts, business assets, overseas property, or blended families — expect to pay £500–£1,000 or more. Most solicitors charge by the hour (typically £150–£350 per hour), which means costs can escalate if your circumstances are complicated.

Why are solicitor wills so expensive?

Solicitors carry significant overheads: office rent, professional indemnity insurance, support staff, and regulatory compliance costs. These are passed on to clients. You are also paying for face-to-face time, which is convenient but not strictly necessary for straightforward wills.

Are online wills legally valid?

Yes. A will prepared online is legally valid in England and Wales provided it meets the requirements of the Wills Act 1837:

  • You must be 18 or over and of sound mind.
  • The will must be in writing and signed by you.
  • Your signature must be witnessed by two people who are present at the same time.
  • Both witnesses must also sign the will.

How the will was drafted — whether by a solicitor, an online service, or yourself — does not affect its legal validity. What matters is how it is signed and witnessed.

Can I write a will for free?

There are some free options, though they come with important caveats.

Free Will Month (October each year) sees participating solicitors draft simple wills at no charge, in the hope you'll make a charitable donation. This is a legitimate option, but availability is limited and you may wait several weeks.

DIY will kits from stationery shops cost £10–£30. These provide blank templates that you fill in yourself. While they can produce a legally valid will, they carry a higher risk of errors — ambiguous wording, missing clauses, or incorrect execution can all lead to disputes after your death.

Charity will schemes offered by organisations like Age UK, the RSPB, or Cancer Research UK provide free or subsidised wills in exchange for considering a charitable legacy.

For most people, spending £69–£150 on a professionally reviewed will is a sensible middle ground between free templates and expensive solicitors.

What affects the cost of a will?

Several factors influence how much you'll pay:

Complexity of your estate. A straightforward will leaving everything to a spouse and then children is simpler (and cheaper) than one involving trusts, business interests, foreign property, or step-children.

Type of will. A single will is cheaper than mirror wills for couples. A will that includes trust provisions (e.g. a property protection trust) costs more than a basic will.

Location. Solicitor fees are generally higher in London and the South East than elsewhere in the UK.

Method of drafting. Face-to-face solicitor appointments are the most expensive option. Online services and telephone-based will writers are significantly cheaper.

Additional services. Some providers charge extra for will storage, amendments, or advice on inheritance tax planning. At Clear Legacy, signing instructions are included in the base price and one amendment is free.

How much does probate cost?

If you're thinking about will costs, it's worth understanding probate fees too. Probate is the legal process of administering someone's estate after death.

The court fee for a Grant of Probate is currently £300 (or £0 for estates under £5,000). On top of this, professional probate services charge either a fixed fee or a percentage of the estate.

  • Fixed-fee probate services (like Clear Legacy): from £495
  • Solicitor probate (percentage-based): typically 1–4% of the estate value
  • Bank probate services: often 3–5% of the estate value

On a £300,000 estate, a solicitor charging 2% would cost £6,000. A fixed-fee service at £495–£1,500 represents a significant saving.

Is it worth paying for a will?

The short answer: yes, almost always.

Dying without a valid will (known as dying "intestate") means your estate is distributed according to the Intestacy Rules — not according to your wishes. Under these rules:

  • Unmarried partners receive nothing, regardless of how long you've been together.
  • Step-children are excluded.
  • Your estate may not pass to the people you would choose.
  • The process takes longer and costs more.

For £69–£150, a professionally drafted will gives you certainty that your wishes will be carried out. Given that 60% of UK adults don't have a will, this is one of the most cost-effective pieces of financial planning you can do.

How to save money on your will

  1. Use an online service. You'll get the same legal outcome as a solicitor at a fraction of the cost.
  2. Get mirror wills as a couple. The per-person cost is lower than two separate single wills.
  3. Keep it simple. If your circumstances are straightforward, you don't need a specialist.
  4. Review rather than rewrite. If your circumstances change slightly, an amendment (codicil) is cheaper than a brand new will.
  5. Act now. Will costs tend to rise over time — and the cost of not having a will can be far higher.
