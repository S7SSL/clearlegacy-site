Mirror Wills UK — Matching Wills for Couples

Updated May 2026 · 6 min read

Mirror wills are two near-identical wills made by a couple — each leaving everything to the other and then on to the same beneficiaries (usually children). They are the simplest, most affordable way for spouses, civil partners and long-term partners to coordinate their wishes. What Are Mirror Wills?

Mirror wills are two separate, legally valid wills that mirror each other's wishes. A typical pair reads: "I leave everything to my partner. If they have already died, I leave everything to our children in equal shares." The second will says exactly the same thing, with the names reversed.

Crucially, they are two independent documents. Each person owns and controls their own will. Either party can change or revoke theirs at any time — including after the first partner dies — without notifying the other.

Mirror Wills vs Mutual Wills

People often confuse these two terms. They are very different.

For the overwhelming majority of UK couples, mirror wills do exactly what is needed. Mutual wills are a niche product that creates lifelong inflexibility — most people who think they want them actually want a life-interest trust, which is also a job for a specialist solicitor.

Heads up: "Mirror" and "mutual" are not interchangeable. If a will writer offers you mutual wills as standard, ask why — it's almost never the right product.

How Mirror Wills Work in Practice

A typical mirror-will pair contains:

  • A revocation clause cancelling any earlier wills
  • Executors — usually each other, plus a backup such as an adult child
  • A primary gift of the entire estate to the other partner
  • Substitute beneficiaries if the partner has already died — typically children in equal shares
  • Guardians for any children under 18
  • Funeral wishes if you want to record them

Each will is signed and witnessed separately under the Wills Act 1837. The two witnesses you use can be the same pair for both wills, but each partner must sign their own document.

Who Should Choose Mirror Wills?

Mirror wills suit most UK couples, including:

  • Married couples and civil partners with shared finances
  • Long-term unmarried partners (especially important — see below)
  • Couples with children together who want everything to pass to the survivor and then to the kids
  • Older couples with grown-up children and grandchildren

Unmarried Couples — Especially Important

Under UK intestacy rules, an unmarried partner inherits nothing automatically, no matter how long you have lived together or whether you have children. Mirror wills are the simplest, fastest fix — without them the estate passes to children, parents or other blood relatives, leaving the surviving partner exposed.

When Mirror Wills May Not Be Right

Consider a specialist solicitor instead if:

  • You have children from previous relationships and want to protect their inheritance after the survivor potentially remarries — a life-interest trust is the usual answer
  • You hold significant business assets and need succession planning
  • Your combined estate is large enough that inheritance tax planning drives the structure
  • You have property abroad in jurisdictions with forced-heirship rules

Frequently Asked Questions

What are mirror wills?

Mirror wills are two separate wills made by a couple that mirror each other's wishes. Each person leaves their estate to the other, with the same substitute beneficiaries — usually their children — if the other has already died. They are independent legal documents and either person can change theirs at any time.

What is the difference between mirror wills and mutual wills?

Mirror wills are independent — either party can change theirs at any time, even after the first partner dies. Mutual wills include a binding legal agreement that prevents the survivor from changing the will after the first death. Mutual wills are rare, complex, and almost always require a solicitor.

Do unmarried couples need mirror wills?

Yes — unmarried couples especially benefit. Under UK intestacy rules unmarried partners inherit nothing automatically, regardless of how long you have lived together. Mirror wills are the simplest way to make sure your partner is provided for.

Can mirror wills include children from previous relationships?

Yes. The questionnaire lets each partner name their own beneficiaries, including children from previous relationships. If you want to ring-fence assets so a stepchild's inheritance is protected after the survivor potentially remarries, a life-interest trust drafted by a specialist solicitor may suit you better.
