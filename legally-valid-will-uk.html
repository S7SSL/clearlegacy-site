What Makes a Will Legally Valid in the UK?

Updated May 2026 · 6 min read

In England and Wales, the legal validity of a will is set by the Wills Act 1837. The rules are short and unambiguous: the will must be in writing, signed by an adult with mental capacity, and witnessed by two non-beneficiary adults present at the same time. Get those right and the will is valid — whether it cost £69 online or £500 at a solicitor.

The Wills Act 1837 — The Five Essential Requirements

Section 9 of the Wills Act 1837 sets out what a valid will must do. In plain English:

  1. It must be in writing. Typed or handwritten — both are fine. Verbal wills, video recordings and audio files are not valid (with a narrow exception for serving armed forces personnel).
  2. You must be 18 or over and have mental capacity. Capacity means understanding what a will does, what your estate consists of, and who might reasonably expect to benefit.
  3. You must sign the will — or direct someone else to sign on your behalf in your presence.
  4. Two adult witnesses must be present at the same time when you sign or acknowledge your signature. They must then sign in your presence.
  5. Witnesses cannot be beneficiaries or the spouse / civil partner of a beneficiary.

Meet all five and the will is legally valid. Miss one and parts of it — or all of it — can fail.

That's it. Probate registries do not check who drafted the document, where it was signed, or how much it cost. They check the five points above.

Mental Capacity — The Banks v Goodfellow Test

"Testamentary capacity" is more specific than general mental capacity. The leading case, Banks v Goodfellow (1870), still applies. To have capacity to make a will you must:

  • Understand the nature of making a will and its effects
  • Understand the extent of the property you are disposing of
  • Be able to comprehend and appreciate the claims to which you ought to give effect (i.e. who might reasonably expect to benefit)
  • Not be suffering from a disorder of the mind that perverts your sense of right

If capacity is in doubt — for example, if you have a recent dementia diagnosis — a doctor's opinion (the "golden rule") is sensible to head off later challenges. This is one situation where a specialist solicitor's involvement is genuinely valuable.

Signing — Get This Right

The signing step is where most DIY wills fail. The rules:

  • You sign the will at the foot, in front of both witnesses, who must be present at the same time
  • Both witnesses then sign in your presence (and ideally in each other's presence)
  • All three signatures should be on the same page if possible
  • Your signature can be a mark (e.g. an X) if you cannot write — but witnesses should record that you understood the document
  • If you physically cannot sign, someone else can sign for you in your presence and at your direction

Who Can — and Can't — Witness a Will

Any adult aged 18 or over with mental capacity can witness, provided they are not a beneficiary or the spouse / civil partner of a beneficiary.

Anyone blind or lacking capacityNo

Witnessing by a beneficiary does not invalidate the entire will — but it voids the gift to that beneficiary, who then takes nothing.

Common Reasons UK Wills Fail

The probate registry sees these issues over and over:

  • Only one witness — or two witnesses not present together
  • A beneficiary witnessed the will — gift to them voided
  • Missing residuary clause — that part of the estate falls under intestacy
  • Ambiguous wording — "to my children" without naming them, "to the cancer charity" without specifying which
  • Signing in the wrong place or initialling rather than signing
  • Two wills found and unclear which is the latest — the later one revokes the earlier, but only if dated
  • Marriage after the will was signed — automatically revokes a will (unless explicitly made in contemplation of marriage)

Things That Don't Affect Validity

Several worries are common but irrelevant:

  • Cost — a £69 will and a £500 will are equally valid
  • Drafting method — handwritten, typed, online-generated all count as "in writing"
  • Storage — keeping the original at home is fine, provided your executor can find it
  • Lack of witnesses' addresses — useful but not required for validity
  • Not having a solicitor involved at any stage

Frequently Asked Questions

What makes a will legally valid in the UK?

In England and Wales, the Wills Act 1837 requires the will to be in writing, made by someone aged 18+ with mental capacity, signed by them, and witnessed by two non-beneficiary adults present at the same time who then sign in the testator's presence.

Does a will need to be signed by a solicitor to be valid?

No. A solicitor's signature is not a legal requirement. A will drafted online and signed at the kitchen table is just as valid as one drafted and signed at a solicitor's office.

Who can witness a will in the UK?

Any adult aged 18 or over with mental capacity, provided they are not a beneficiary or married to a beneficiary. Witnessing by a beneficiary voids the gift to that beneficiary.

Can a will be invalidated after it is signed?

Yes. A will can be challenged on the basis of lack of capacity, undue influence, fraud, forgery, or improper execution. Marriage automatically revokes an existing will (unless made in contemplation of marriage), and a later will revokes an earlier one.

What are the most common reasons a UK will is invalid?

Improper witnessing, beneficiaries acting as witnesses, missing residuary clause, ambiguous wording, and signing in the wrong place. DIY wills fail far more often than wills drafted by a will writing service.
