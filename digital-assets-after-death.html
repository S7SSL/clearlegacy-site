What happens to digital assets when you die?

England & Wales · After Death · Digital

Quick answer

Digital assets — online accounts, photos, email, social media, cryptocurrency, domains and loyalty points — don't automatically pass under a will, and access is governed by each platform's terms. Plan ahead: list your digital assets, record how they're accessed securely, and use platforms' legacy/memorialisation tools. Never put passwords in your will (it becomes public after probate); use a separate secure record instead.

Detailed explanation

Digital legacy is the fastest-growing gap in estate planning.

  • Inventory: list accounts, photos, crypto, domains, subscriptions and points.
  • Access: keep a secure, separate record of how to access them — not in the will.
  • Use platform tools: legacy contacts and memorialisation settings on major services.
  • Crypto: without the keys, the asset is lost — document access securely.
  • Your will can name who should deal with your digital estate.
Example scenario

Mia lists her digital life — cloud photos, two email accounts, a crypto wallet and several subscriptions — and stores access details with a secure password manager, naming a digital executor in her will. Her family can find and manage everything, and her crypto isn't lost.

Frequently asked questions

What happens to digital assets when you die?

Digital assets don't automatically pass under a will, and access depends on each platform's terms. Plan by listing them, recording secure access separately, and using legacy or memorialisation tools. Never put passwords in your will, as it becomes public after probate.

Should I put my passwords in my will?

No. A will becomes a public document after probate, so passwords or keys in it would be exposed. Keep them in a separate, secure record and refer to its existence — not its contents — in your planning.
