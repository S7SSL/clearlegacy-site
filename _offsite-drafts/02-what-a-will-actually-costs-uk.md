The cost of writing a will in the UK ranges from about £20 to £400. That's a 20× spread for what is, legally, the same product. So what are you actually paying for?

## The price spread, in plain numbers (May 2026)

- **DIY paper kit (WHSmith etc.):** £20–£40
- **Online will service (ClearLegacy):** £69 single / £99 mirror
- **Online will service with phone consultation (Farewill):** £100 single / £165 mirror
- **Co-op Legal Services:** £150+ single / £245+ mirror
- **High-street solicitor:** £150–£400 single / £250–£600 mirror

That's the published market. Notice how the legal product — a will that complies with the Wills Act 1837 — appears at every price point. The differences aren't legal; they're operational.

## What makes a will more expensive

**Office rent.** A solicitor on the high street is paying rent on a Georgian building. Every appointment you attend in person is paying for that. An online service doesn't carry that cost.

**Hourly billing.** Solicitors typically charge by the hour. A "simple" will at a high-street firm can quietly drift from £150 to £400 because the clock keeps running through revisions and follow-ups. Fixed-fee online services have a single number that doesn't move.

**Phone consultations.** Some online services include a 30-minute phone call as standard. That's roughly £30 of staff cost embedded in the price. If you genuinely value the phone contact, it's worth paying. If you'd rather just fill in a form, you're paying for something you didn't want.

**Subscriptions.** A few providers offer ongoing storage or "unlimited update" subscriptions at a few pounds a month. Over five or ten years that quietly doubles the original cost. One-off pricing is usually better value if you're going to amend your will once or twice in your lifetime.

**Wider practice areas.** A solicitor who also does conveyancing, divorce and commercial leases brings expertise you don't need for a straightforward will. For complex estates that's worth paying for. For simple wills it isn't.

## What makes a will cheaper

The cheapest legally valid will is a DIY paper kit at around £20. That's also where wills fail in probate at the highest rate — usually because of witnessing or signature errors that a review process would have caught.

The cheapest *reviewed* will is around £69. Below that, you're trading away professional review, which is where most of the value sits.

## What you should actually pay

For a straightforward estate — homeowner, named beneficiaries, no unusual circumstances — anything above £100 is mostly paying for overhead. £69 buys you the same legal product at the same Wills Act 1837 standard, with professional review and a 24-hour turnaround.

For a complex estate — discretionary trusts, foreign property, contested family — pay for a solicitor. The £300 isn't for the will; it's for the legal expertise around it.

Don't pay £20 unless you really know what you're doing. The £49 saving versus a £69 reviewed will isn't worth the probate risk.

## The bottom line

The legal product is the same at every price. The difference is operational overhead. Pick the price point that matches what you actually need: review and reliability, not office furniture.
