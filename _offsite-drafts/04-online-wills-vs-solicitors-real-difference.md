The market for UK will writing is quietly polarising. On one end, [online wills vs solicitors](https://www.clearlegacy.co.uk/online-will-vs-solicitor/) is one of the most-searched will-related queries in Google. On the other, the actual difference between the two products — at the legal level — is essentially zero. So what are people really comparing?

## The legal product is identical

Section 9 of the Wills Act 1837 sets out a short list of requirements for any valid will in England and Wales. Both online wills and solicitor wills must comply with the same law. Both produce documents the Probate Registry treats identically. Both are challenged on the same grounds — capacity, undue influence, fraud, or improper execution. The drafting route doesn't affect any of that.

If you ask "is my online will going to hold up?" the legal answer is: yes, in exactly the same way a solicitor will would. If a [legally valid online will](https://www.clearlegacy.co.uk/legally-valid-will-uk/) is signed in the presence of two adult witnesses (who are not beneficiaries), it has the full force of any other will under UK law.

## When a solicitor is actually the right choice

For some estates, a solicitor genuinely earns the fee. Use a solicitor if:

- You have **discretionary trusts** for vulnerable beneficiaries
- You own **foreign property** in jurisdictions with different succession rules
- You have **business interests with shareholder agreements**
- You're in a **contested family situation** (estranged children, blended families with disputes)
- You need **active inheritance tax planning** beyond what a will can do

For everyone else — and that's most UK adults — an [affordable online will](https://www.clearlegacy.co.uk/cheap-will-uk/) at £69 is the same legal product. A reputable online will service flags during review if your situation needs a solicitor and refers you out.

## The honest comparison

The "online wills vs solicitors" question is really a question about overhead, not law. Solicitors carry expensive overhead structures that are necessary for some legal work and unnecessary for routine will writing. Online services strip out that overhead. The will itself — the bit that the Probate Registry actually cares about — is the same document at either price.

For a straightforward estate, paying £400 for a will is paying £331 for the office furniture. For a complex estate, that £400 buys actual legal advice you can't get from an online service. Knowing which category you're in is the only question that matters.
