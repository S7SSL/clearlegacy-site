There is a question I get asked more than any other when people are thinking about writing a will: "is an online will actually legal in the UK?"

The short answer is yes. The longer answer is more interesting.

## The law cares about the document, not the drafter

In England and Wales, the legal status of a will is governed by section 9 of the Wills Act 1837. It sets out five requirements:

1. The will is in writing
2. The testator is aged 18 or over and has testamentary capacity
3. The will is signed by the testator
4. The signature is made in the presence of two witnesses present at the same time
5. Each witness then signs in the presence of the testator

That is the entire legal test. There is no requirement that a solicitor draft the will, no requirement that the will be filed anywhere, and no requirement that it follow a particular template. The law is interested in the document and the way it was signed — nothing else.

## So why does anyone use a solicitor?

Three reasons, and they're all practical rather than legal:

**Office overhead.** Solicitors operate from offices that need rent. You're paying for that overhead in every fee.

**Hourly billing.** A 45-minute appointment at £200/hour is £150 just for the meeting, before drafting begins.

**Wider expertise.** A high-street firm does conveyancing, divorce, commercial leases and wills. For complex estates with discretionary trusts or foreign property, that breadth genuinely matters. For a straightforward will, it doesn't.

## Where online wills genuinely meet the same standard

A reputable online will service does three things that matter:

1. **Drafts from a structured questionnaire** that covers every Wills Act 1837 requirement
2. **Has a qualified person review the draft** before it's released
3. **Provides clear signing and witnessing instructions** — because that's where most wills fail in probate

If those three boxes are ticked, the legal product is identical to a solicitor-drafted will. The Probate Registry treats them the same. The Grant of Probate is identical.

## Where online wills are wrong

Some estates need a solicitor. If you have:

- A discretionary trust for a vulnerable beneficiary
- Foreign property in jurisdictions with different succession rules
- A family business with shareholder agreements
- A blended family with contested inheritance claims
- An estate that genuinely needs active inheritance tax planning beyond what a will can do

…then a solicitor is the right choice. A good online will service will tell you this during review and refer you out, rather than sell you a product that doesn't fit.

## The bottom line

For the vast majority of UK adults — homeowners, couples, parents — an online will is the right product. It costs a fraction of a solicitor's fee, takes 15 minutes to complete, and produces a legally identical document. The savings are real, the legal validity is identical, and the only thing you give up is an in-person meeting.

The Wills Act 1837 doesn't care whether the will was drafted online or in a Georgian office in a market town. It only cares that the document is properly written, properly signed, and properly witnessed.
