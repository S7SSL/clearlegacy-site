## What the law actually requires

Section 9 of the Wills Act 1837 sets out the entire legal test for a valid will in England and Wales:

1. The will is in writing
2. The testator is aged 18 or over and has testamentary capacity
3. The will is signed by the testator
4. The signature is made or acknowledged in the presence of two witnesses present at the same time
5. Each witness signs in the presence of the testator

That's the law. There is no requirement for a solicitor to be involved. There is no requirement for the will to follow a particular template. There is no requirement for the will to be filed anywhere. The Probate Registry, when granting probate, checks the document against these five requirements — nothing more.

A [legally valid online will](https://www.clearlegacy.co.uk/legally-valid-will-uk/) meets every requirement. So does a solicitor will. They are legally indistinguishable in probate.

## Where online wills genuinely meet the same standard

A reputable online will service does three things that matter:

1. **Drafts from a structured questionnaire** that covers every Wills Act 1837 requirement
2. **Has a qualified person review the draft** before it is released
3. **Provides clear signing and witnessing instructions** — because that is where most wills fail in probate

If those three boxes are ticked, the legal product is identical. Probate registries process online wills the same as solicitor-drafted wills.

## Where online wills are wrong

Some estates need a solicitor. If you have a discretionary trust for a vulnerable beneficiary, foreign property in jurisdictions with different succession rules, a family business with shareholder agreements, or a contested family situation — a solicitor is the right choice. A good online will service tells you this during review and refers you out.

## What you actually pay extra for at a solicitor

Three things, and they are all practical rather than legal:

- **Office overhead.** Solicitors operate from offices that need rent. You pay for that overhead in every fee.
- **Hourly billing.** A 45-minute appointment at £200/hour is £150 just for the meeting, before drafting begins.
- **Wider expertise.** A high-street firm does conveyancing, divorce, commercial leases and wills. For complex estates that breadth genuinely matters. For a straightforward will, it does not.
